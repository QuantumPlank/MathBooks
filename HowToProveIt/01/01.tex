\section{Deductive Reasoning and Logical Connectives}

\begin{definition}[Premises]
    A \textbf{premise} is a a true or false statement (a proposition).
\end{definition}

\begin{definition}[Conclusions]
    A \textbf{conclusion} is a special type of proposition whose truth value is dependent on other propositions.
\end{definition}

We arrive at a \textit{conclusion} from the assumption that some other premises are true.

\begin{definition}[Arguments]
    An \textbf{argument} is one or more premises directed towards arriving at a logical conclusion.
\end{definition}

We will say that an argument is \textbf{valid} if the premises cannot all be true without the conclusion being true as well.

In order to combine statements to form more complex statements we can use \textbf{connective symbols} to stand for words used to combine statements.

\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{c | c}
        Symbol  & Meaning           \\
        \hline
        $\land$ & and (conjunction) \\
        $lor$   & or (disjunction)  \\
        $\lnot$ & not (negation)
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}
